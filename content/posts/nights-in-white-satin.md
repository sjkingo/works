There are letters you write that you never send. Not because you lose your
nerve, but because the act of writing them was the point. The pen moving across
the page was the conversation. The words were never meant for the person they
were addressed to. They were meant for the version of you that still believed
saying the right thing could bridge the distance between one mind and another.

This is the territory, the space between what you feel and what you can make
another person understand. The gap that looks narrow enough to step across but
turns out to be an ocean every time you try. You have stood on your side of it
so many times you know the shoreline by heart. The exact place where language
runs out and all that remains is the ache of almost. You think just what you
want to be, and then the thought dissolves the moment you try to hand it to
someone else.

We move through the world wrapped in experience that is entirely our own. Not
approximately our own. Entirely. The shade of red you see is not the shade of
red I see, and neither of us will ever know the difference. We are locked
inside our own perceptions the way a body is locked inside its skin, pressing
up against the boundary, certain that the person beside us must feel the same
warmth, the same weight, the same strange beauty in a night that seems to go on
forever. But certainty is not the same as truth. It never was.

Some truths arrive the way morning does. Not all at once, but as a slow
withdrawal of darkness, each moment indistinguishable from the last until
suddenly you can see. You cannot point to the instant it changed. You only know
that something you were sure of yesterday feels naive today, and something you
dismissed last year now sits at the centre of everything. Your thoughts decide
what they want to show you on their own schedule. You are not the curator of
your own mind. You are the visitor.

The nights are the worst. Not because they are lonely, although they are. But
because they are beautiful. That is the cruelty no one warns you about. The way
solitude sharpens everything. Moonlight looks different when you are carrying
the full weight of your own consciousness with no one to distribute it to.
Music sounds different. Satin feels different against skin that has stopped
pretending it does not want to be touched. The world becomes almost unbearably
vivid, as though isolation has cleaned the glass you were looking through and
now you can see every detail of the life you are living but cannot translate.

And the people around you, the ones who say they understand, they cannot. Not
because they are unkind, but because understanding requires having stood in the
exact same room, and no two people ever stand in the same room. They see your
face. They do not see the weather behind it. They hear your voice and assume
the words are the message, when really the message is everything you left
between the lines. The pause before you answered. The breath you took that was
not quite deep enough. They love you, some of them. And love, it turns out,
does not require comprehension. It only requires presence. Which is both the
most beautiful and the most devastating thing about it.

Truth is a strange companion in all of this. It does not comfort. It does not
heal. It simply sits beside you, indifferent to whether you acknowledge it or
not. Just what the truth is, you cannot always say. It shifts depending on the
hour, the light, how much silence you have let accumulate. But you know it is
there the way you know your own pulse. Not because you can see it, but because
everything else moves in time with it.

The letters pile up in a drawer no one opens. And that is fine. They were never
meant to arrive. They were only ever meant to prove that you were here, that
you felt this, that the night's quiet meant something, even if meaning is the
one thing that cannot survive the journey from one person to another.

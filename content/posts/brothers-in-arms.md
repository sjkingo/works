[There is a scene in Miami Vice](https://youtu.be/zgTpW9wJGVg?si=wxHVsvnoONQj3dsF) that no one who has seen it ever quite shakes
loose. Crockett behind the wheel, the Ferrari cutting through a darkness that
feels less like night and more like a state of mind. No dialogue. No plot. Just
Mark Knopfler's guitar, threading its way through the silence like something
that has been waiting a very long time to be said.

The song is not about war. Not really. It borrows the language of soldiers and
battlefields, but what it actually describes is something far older and more
private: the weight of having stood beside someone through things that cannot
be explained to anyone who was not there. The loyalty that does not announce
itself. The kind that builds walls and never asks why.

And the man carrying all of it is tired. You can hear it in Knopfler's voice,
which does not so much sing as exhale. There is no performance in it. No
reaching for the rafters. Just a man talking quietly in a room he assumes is
empty, saying the things you only say when you have stopped caring whether
anyone hears. The fatigue is not entirely physical. It is the kind that settles
into the very being of a person, into the way they hold their shoulders, the
way they look at a horizon they once believed they could reach but know now they
never will.

That is what the Miami Vice scene understood. It did not use the song as
background music. It let the song become the scene. The car moving through the
dark was the song made visible. A man in motion with nowhere particular to go,
carried forward by momentum and habit and something too deep to call sadness,
because sadness implies you still have the energy to feel it. This was
something quieter than that. Something that had already made its peace.

Knopfler's guitar does the rest. That tone. Clean and unhurried, every note
given room to breathe and fade, like cigarette smoke in still air. It does not
build toward anything. It simply exists, the way grief exists once you stop
fighting it. There is no resolution in the song because there is no resolution
in the thing it describes. You carry what you carry. You stand where you stood.
And one day you sit down, not because the fight is over, but because you have
been standing for so long that sitting is its own kind of courage.

The scene ends the way the best ones do. Not with an answer. Just the road, the
dark, and the sound of someone who gave everything he had and then kept going
anyway, because that is what loyalty looks like when it has nothing left to
prove.
